SPEAKERS

 

Our speakers include the following researchers.


Madan Musuvathi, Microsoft Research.

Dr Musuvathi is a researcher in the Software Reliability Research group at Microsoft Research. He is interested in the analysis of complex and large-scale systems. His research is inter-disciplinary and includes systems, networking, program analysis, model checking, verification, and theorem proving. He obtained his M.S. and Ph.D. at Stanford University, where he worked under the guidance of Profs Dill and Engler.

Mark Moir, Sun Microsystems

Dr. Moir joined Sun Microsystems Laboratories in 2000, where he is now a Distinguished Engineer and Principal Investigator of the Scalable Synchronization Research Group. Dr. Moir's main research interests concern practical and theoretical aspects of concurrent, distributed, and real-time systems, particularly hardware and software support for programming constructs that facilitate scalable synchronization in shared memory multiprocessors.

G. Ramalingam, Microsoft Research

Dr. Ramalingam is a member of the Rigorous Software Engineering Group at Microsoft Research India. He is interested in the areas of Programming Languages and Software Engineering, in making software development easier, more enjoyable, more reliable, more productive. Some of his more specific interests include static program analysis and programming tools.

Vijay Saraswat, Ibm Research

Vijay Saraswat joined IBM Research in 2003, after a year as a Professor at Penn State, a couple of years at startups and 13 years at Xerox PARC and AT&T Research. His main interests are in programming languages, constraints, logic and concurrency. At IBM, he leads the work on the design of X10, a modern object-oriented programming language intended for scalable concurrent computing.  

Peter Sewell, CAmbridge University.

Peter Sewell is a Reader and EPSRC Leadership Fellow at the University of Cambridge Computer Laboratory.  His research aims to build rigorous foundations for the engineering of real-world computer systems, to make them better-understood, more robust, and more secure. This applied semantics work involves programming languages, network protocols, and security, and currently is focussed on the relaxed memory models of multiprocessors and concurrent languages.

Satnam Singh, Microsoft Research.

Satnam Singh's research involves finding novel ways to program and use FPGAs. He is interested in making the circuits on these chips change as they run to adapt to new situations. He holds a PhD from the University of Glasgow. He worked at Xilinx lab where we developed tools and technology for designing and formally verifying circuits for FPGAs. In 2004 he joined Microsoft where we worked on techniques for producing concurrent and parallel programs. In 2006 he moved to Microsoft's research laboratory in Cambridge where he works on reconfigurable computing and parallel functional programming.

Matthew Parkinson, Cambridge University

Dr. Parkinson is a Royal Ac     ademy of Engineering and EPSRC Research Fellow in the Cambridge University Computer Lab. His research interests include program verification, separation logic and object-oriented programming.

Jean Pierre Talpin, INRIA

Dr. Talpin is Senior Researcher at INRIA and leads the Espresso team-project at IRISA. He is interested in models of computations, formal methods and automated code generation technique for computer-aided engineering embedded software. In this aim, his team, Espresso, develops an integrated development environment and technology demonstrator based on a multi-clocked synchronous model of computation of the data-flow specification language, Signal. Polychrony comprises a reflexive graphical editor using Eclipse as part of the OpenEmbeDD platform.

Adam Welc, Intel Research.

Dr. Welc joined Intel's Programing Systems Lab in March 2006 where he currently holds a position of Senior Research Scientist. His work is in the area of programming language design and implementation, with specific interests in concurrency control, compiler and run-time system optimizations, transactional processing as well as  architectural support for programming languages and applications.
